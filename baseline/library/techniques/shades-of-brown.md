# Designing with brown shades — applied rules

Core idea: brown is a warm neutral (a deep, desaturated orange). It reads as natural, grounded, reliable, and premium. Use it as a base/ground rather than an accent, then let one cooler or brighter color do the talking. Browns split into three tiers — light (base/background), medium (warm energy/headlines), dark (hierarchy/refinement). Pick the tier by job, not by taste.

## Build decisions

1. **Use brown as the GROUND, not the accent.** Brown's strength is being a warm neutral base that "easily complements other colors." Make it the surface/background, then add one vivid accent on top. Avoid filling a whole layout with brown — too much "can make designs look dull or overly rustic."

2. **Match the tier to the role.** Light browns = approachable backgrounds and large surfaces. Medium browns = warmth and emphasis (headlines, energetic blocks, attention-grabbing CTAs). Dark browns = hierarchy, refinement, and the elements you most want users to act on.

3. **Pick the shade by brand voice.** Traditional / heritage / premium → richer, darker browns (mocha #6D3B07, chocolate #7B3F00). Modern / youthful / airy → lighter tints (beige #EDE8D0, nude #F7D9BC, tan #D6B588). Don't default to "a brown" — choose along the light↔dark axis to match identity.

4. **Pair brown with a cool or bright complement, deliberately.** Proven contrasts from the source: light brown base + navy blue or olive-green accent; copper/orange-brown + turquoise or teal. The job of the accent is to break the warm monotone and add a focal point.

5. **Monochromatic brown is a valid scheme — for calm.** Stacking tints/shades of brown alone (light surface → medium mid → dark text) yields a "cozy, calming" mood. Reach for this on wellness, leisure, travel, home/furniture, and eco/wholesome brands. Add at most a single small pop of color to keep it from going flat.

6. **Lean on the meaning, don't fight it.** Brown signals safety, stability, strength, reliability, trust, nature, and (in deep/rich tiers) luxury. Use it when those are the message; pick a different palette if you need playful, techy, or high-energy.

7. **Treat brown text/background as a contrast decision, not a given.** Brown can give "good contrast with white or black text" — but only certain shades. Always run a contrast check (target WCAG AA: 4.5:1 body, 3:1 large text) before trusting a brown-on-light or text-on-brown pairing. A mid-tone brown on cream often fails body-text contrast even when it looks fine.

## Named shades (hex) by tier — reusable starting points

- Light (surfaces/backgrounds): Beige #EDE8D0 · Nude #F7D9BC · Sand #CBBD93 · Tan #D6B588
- Medium (warmth/emphasis/CTA): Bronze #CE8946 · Burnt sienna #E97451 · Cinnamon #D47E30 · Copper #C68346 · Sepia #704214
- Dark (hierarchy/text/refinement): Chocolate #7B3F00 · Mocha #6D3B07 · Umber #635147 · Taupe #54463A

Tip: Nude #F7D9BC works as a subtle state cue (hover/active/completed) on otherwise solid elements. Taupe #54463A (gray mixed into brown) is the most "neutral/professional" pick for nav bars and body type that must let other colors lead.

## Build checklist
- [ ] Is brown the ground/base, with at most one vivid accent on top (not brown everywhere)?
- [ ] Does each brown's tier (light/medium/dark) match its job (surface / emphasis / hierarchy)?
- [ ] Does the shade's darkness match the brand voice (heritage→dark, modern→light)?
- [ ] If not monochromatic, is there a deliberate cool/bright complement (navy, olive, teal) creating a focal point?
- [ ] Did I run an actual contrast check on every text-on-brown and brown-on-light pairing (AA 4.5:1 body / 3:1 large)?
- [ ] Are medium/dark browns reserved to mark the most important action or next step?
