# Best fonts for resumes — applied type decisions

Core idea: for a text-first document, the typeface must disappear and let content read. Choose for legibility at small sizes, neutral professional tone, full character/weight coverage, and machine-parseability — not for flair. Match the family's personality to the industry, then enforce a strict size/weight hierarchy. All picks below are free Google Fonts (web-safe, render identically across systems and PDF export).

## Named picks by job (each = a default you can reach for)

### Sans serif — modern, clean, tech/creative/digital
1. **Inter** — built for screens, large x-height, optimized spacing; reads at every size on mobile or print. Default for tech, UX/UI, remote/clarity-first work. (This is also the TESQ house face — safe, neutral, legible.)
2. **Roboto** — mechanical precision + humanist curves; modern and approachable. Default for software/product roles.
3. **Open Sans** — friendly, neutral, balanced x-height and counters; the safe all-industry default when unsure.
4. **Source Sans Pro** — clean, technical, even stroke widths; subtle and polished for design/writing roles.
5. **Work Sans** / **DM Sans** — on-screen-optimized geometrics with generous spacing; scannable at small sizes for startups, PM, dev, digital-marketing.
6. **Lato** / **Nunito Sans** — semi-rounded, warm, human; pick when you want approachable over clinical (comms, education, nonprofit, customer service).
7. **IBM Plex Sans** / **Fira Sans** — rational, engineered, expressive; competence-signalling for engineering/data/technical fields.
8. **Libre Franklin** / **Montserrat** — strong, confident, geometric; use for headings/branding flair in business, marketing, creative-director roles (Montserrat has the most personality — use it deliberately, not by accident).
9. **Noto Sans** — near-universal glyph coverage; choose for multilingual / international / global-context documents where missing characters would break the layout.
10. **Raleway** — sleek geometric with stylistic alternates; **headings/accents only**, not body — too thin and stylized for paragraph text.

### Serif — traditional, formal, authoritative
11. **Merriweather** / **PT Serif** / **Domine** — serifs explicitly engineered for screen readability (large x-height, robust serifs). Choose these serifs when the document is read on-screen, not just printed.
12. **Lora** / **Crimson Text** — literary, refined, modern-classic; publishing, education, humanities, editorial.
13. **EB Garamond** / **Cormorant Garamond** / **Baskerville** — high-contrast classical faces for authority and heritage (law, finance, academia, executive). Note: high stroke contrast + delicate serifs degrade at small sizes — keep these at or above 11pt and avoid for tiny captions; Cormorant especially is display-leaning.

### Slab serif
14. **Arvo** — bold monolinear slab; strong structural presence that reinforces clearly-sectioned, hierarchy-driven layouts (engineering, data, structured docs).

## Build decisions
- **Match face to industry tone.** Sans = modern/tech/creative; serif = formal/traditional (law, finance, academia, medicine). Don't put a geometric display sans on a law-firm document, or a high-contrast Garamond on a startup one.
- **Pick a face with a full family.** Require regular + bold + italic at minimum so hierarchy comes from weight/style within ONE family — never mix fonts to fake emphasis.
- **Enforce the size ladder** (from the article): body **10–12pt**, headings/section titles **14–18pt**, name/title largest. Never drop body under 10pt (unreadable) or push it over 12pt (bulky/unpolished).
- **Demand complete character support** — punctuation, numbers, symbols, accents — so no glyph falls back to a mismatched substitute.
- **Stay machine-parseable (ATS/PDF):** avoid faces with unusual ligatures, embedded symbols, or graphical quirks; pick straightforward typefaces so automated parsers and any system render them correctly. Embed/subset fonts in PDF; never rely on a rare/custom font being installed on the reader's machine.
- **Prefer low-contrast, simple shapes for body.** Minimal thick/thin variation and clean counters read better and scale down better than ornamental or high-contrast faces.
- **Reserve display/geometric faces (Raleway, Cormorant, Montserrat) for headings/accents**, keep a calm, high-legibility face for body.
- **Hard avoid:** script, decorative, and novelty faces — Comic Sans, Papyrus, or anything overly stylized. They distract, hurt scanning, and read as unprofessional.

## Build checklist
- [ ] Face matches the document's industry tone (sans=modern, serif=formal)?
- [ ] One family with regular/bold/italic — hierarchy from weight, not from a second font?
- [ ] Body 10–12pt, headings 14–18pt, name largest — size ladder enforced?
- [ ] Body face is legible, simple, low-contrast at small size (display faces confined to headings)?
- [ ] Full glyph coverage (numbers, punctuation, accents) and web-safe/Google Font for cross-system + PDF fidelity?
- [ ] ATS/parser-safe (no exotic ligatures or embedded symbols); fonts subset/embedded in the PDF?
- [ ] No script/decorative/novelty face anywhere?
