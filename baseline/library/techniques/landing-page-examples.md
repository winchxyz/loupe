# Landing page patterns — applied build decisions

Core idea: a landing page is NOT a homepage. Every block exists to move the visitor toward ONE action. Strip anything that competes with that goal. The patterns below are reusable across page types; pick the ones that fit the goal and skip the rest.

## Reusable patterns (each = a build decision + when to use)

1. **One-goal layout** — Decide the single conversion action BEFORE designing, then subordinate every section to it. Avoid: secondary CTAs, multi-purpose hero, "explore everything" nav. *When:* always — this is the gate the rest of the page passes through.

2. **Hero = headline + one strong visual that states value instantly** — Lead with a compelling headline and a single dominant hero visual (product mockup, animated GIF showing it in use, or a recognizable real photo). The hero must communicate core value before any scroll. Choose a soft secondary action ("Discover More") only when the product needs to be understood before commitment. *When:* every page; tighten the headline to the single biggest problem solved (SaaS/app pattern).

3. **Stripped navigation** — Minimize or remove top-level nav so there's no escape hatch from the funnel. Avoid full site menus on a landing page. *When:* any conversion-first page; relax only if the page doubles as a hub.

4. **Primary CTA, prominent and above the fold** — Make the CTA button visually dominant via contrasting color; use action-oriented verb copy ("Start Your Free Trial", "Get a Quote", "Enroll Now"), never "Submit". Place a primary CTA above the fold and repeat it down-page at decision points. *When:* always.

5. **Friction-removal entry point** — Offer a low-commitment first step: free trial / free tier, 7-day pass, complimentary session, downloadable checklist or audit as a lead magnet. Capture the lead at that easy step. *When:* the ask is high-commitment (gym, SaaS, agency, financial services).

6. **Trust-signal cluster near the ask** — Place social proof beside the CTA/form: testimonials and named quotes, star ratings, client/company logos, accreditation/security badges, real headshots + bios, and a privacy-policy link for sensitive forms. *When:* always; weight it heaviest for trust-sensitive verticals (legal, financial, childcare).

7. **Outcome/transformation framing, not feature lists** — Sell the result the visitor gets, not the spec sheet. Reframe service names as solved problems ("We help teams fix inconsistent messaging" > "Branding services"). Highlight the transformation, not just amenities. *When:* services and agencies.

8. **Message-match to the inbound source** — Mirror the copy/offer of the ad or link that brought the visitor in. Mismatch spikes bounce; consistency keeps trust and conversion high. *When:* any page fed by paid ads or campaign links.

9. **Interactive lead-capture device** — Embed a quiz, calculator, availability checker, or "find near you" tool that gives the visitor value AND captures the lead in one move. *When:* the offer is configurable or the visitor needs help self-qualifying (financial, gym, beverage retail).

10. **Image-forward / story layout** — When the product IS the appeal (e-commerce, collectibles, fashion, florist, landscaping), go image-forward with high-res photography, 360/video, and generous white space so products take center stage; add a short narrative hook ("Curator's Note", origin story). *When:* visual or lifestyle products.

11. **Urgency mechanics** — Add a countdown timer, early-bird pricing with a visible expiration, same-day cutoff times, or limited-drop framing to drive a fast decision. *When:* events, sales/drops, registration deadlines.

12. **Logistics-clarity block** — For events/services, surface the practical blockers up front: date/time/location, map + parking, delivery radius, areas served, schedule. Removing logistical friction is itself a conversion lever. *When:* events, local services, anything location-bound.

13. **Strict visual hierarchy** — Drive the eye with size, color, spacing, and font weight so it flows value-prop -> CTA -> supporting features in a logical order. The headline and CTA must be the MOST visually dominant elements; supporting detail must read as subordinate. *When:* always — this is what makes the one-goal layout actually scan.

## Build checklist
- [ ] One conversion goal named, and every section earns its place against it?
- [ ] Hero states value in one headline + one dominant visual, no clutter?
- [ ] Top nav minimized/removed so the funnel has no leaks?
- [ ] Primary CTA above the fold, contrasting color, verb-first copy (not "Submit")?
- [ ] Trust signals (testimonials/logos/badges/privacy link) sit next to the ask?
- [ ] Copy matches the ad/link that brought the visitor in?
- [ ] Hierarchy makes headline + CTA the most dominant elements; mobile checked?

> Ties to our experience: the "clear visual hierarchy" rule (size/spacing/weight guiding the eye in order) is the same discipline our PAIN.md hierarchy bugs broke — the per-cell stagger that read as crooked (common-fate violation) and the repos-table header misalignment (broken column similarity/continuity); on a landing page that same disorder reads as a leaky funnel, not just an ugly grid.
