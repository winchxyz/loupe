# Triadic color schemes — applied rules

Core idea: a triad is three hues spaced 120° apart on the wheel (an equilateral triangle). It gives you contrast and vibrancy without the head-on tension of a complementary pair. Use it when you want more than one accent but the palette still has to read as a deliberate system.

## The build decisions
1. Pick ONE dominant hue first, driven by brand meaning (e.g. green = growth/abundance), then derive the other two as its 120° neighbors. Don't choose three colors you "like" independently — the even spacing is what makes it harmonious.
2. Choose the triad TIER by how loud you want the design:
   - Primary triad (red / yellow / blue) → maximum contrast, high vibrancy. Use for playful, energetic, attention-first UI.
   - Secondary triad (orange / green / violet) → more subtle than primary. Use for friendly-but-grounded brands.
   - Tertiary triads (red-orange / yellow-green / blue-violet, OR yellow-orange / blue-green / red-violet) → the most understated. Use when you want hue variety but a calmer, more sophisticated feel.
   Default toward a tertiary triad for product/editorial work; reserve the primary triad for genuinely bold, poster-like contexts.
3. Apply the 60-30-10 split, not equal thirds. 60% dominant, 30% second hue, 10% third as the accent. Three colors at equal weight fight each other and look amateur — the imbalance is what creates a focal point.
4. Don't use the three hues at full saturation. Knock back two of them via tints/shades/desaturation so one stays the hero; pull the others toward supporting/background roles. Adjust saturation and lightness for cohesion rather than letting all three scream.
5. Reserve the 10% third hue for true accents only — the CTA, an active state, a highlight — never for body text or large fills. Its job is to punctuate.
6. Verify contrast on every real text/background pairing (target WCAG AA: 4.5:1 body, 3:1 large/UI). Triads put saturated hues next to each other, so legibility is the first thing that breaks — check the actual pairs you ship, not the swatches in isolation.
7. If a true triad feels too busy, step down to a nearby relationship instead of forcing it: analogous (calm/unified), complementary (max contrast, two hues), split-complementary (contrast with less intensity), monochromatic (one hue, simplest), or tetradic (four hues, most vivid). Triadic is one point on that spectrum, not the only answer.

## Build checklist
- [ ] Dominant hue chosen from brand meaning, other two derived at 120° (not picked ad hoc)?
- [ ] Triad tier (primary / secondary / tertiary) matched to how loud the design should be?
- [ ] 60-30-10 weighting applied — one clear hero, not three equal colors?
- [ ] Two of the three hues knocked back in saturation/lightness so they support, not compete?
- [ ] Third hue confined to accents (CTA / active state), never body text?
- [ ] Contrast checked on the actual text-on-background pairs that ship (AA)?
