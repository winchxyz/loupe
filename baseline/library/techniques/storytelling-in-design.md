# Storytelling in design — applied rules

Core idea: a product is a story the visitor moves through, like rooms in a building or chapters in a book. Design the *order* and *pacing* of what they meet, not just the screens in isolation. The journey IS the structure.

## The rules (each = a build decision)
1. Map the journey before laying out pixels — list the silent questions a first-time visitor asks in sequence ("Where do I go? Is this the right place? Can I trust this? How long will this take?"), then make sure each screen answers the next one in the order it's met. Avoid designing screens as disconnected artifacts.
2. Open with a welcome that states who/what in one line — lead with logo + name + a plain value sentence ("Find and book hotels in Nigeria"). Choose an explicit self-introduction over a clever-but-mute hero; treat the visitor like a guest you want to return.
3. Use a known arc for the layout spine — for commerce: intro → featured representatives of the offering → simple navigation path → select/customize/checkout/post-purchase → returns & re-visit support. Pick the familiar narrative skeleton over a novel structure unless you have a reason.
4. Reveal over time, don't dump — surface each piece of info at the moment it becomes relevant (security at checkout, shipping when chosen), not all at once on one screen. Choose progressive disclosure; avoid cramming the whole experience into a single dense view.
5. Pace the unfold to the moment of need — sequence reveals to the step the user is on, so each beat lands when it answers a live question rather than pre-loading anxiety.
6. Design by subtraction — "design is as much about what you choose NOT to include." Every element should earn its place by carrying part of the story; cut anything that doesn't advance the journey. Restraint is a design act, not a leftover.
7. Make the journey legible to the team — write the narrative down (why this element, this order) and visualize key moments with a site map so stakeholders grasp the holistic flow and design decisions are justifiable, not improvised.

## Build checklist (use when planning a site's flow)
- [ ] Journey mapped as an ordered list of the visitor's questions, each answered in sequence?
- [ ] Hero states who you are + what you offer in one plain line, above any cleverness?
- [ ] Layout follows a familiar arc (intro → represent → navigate → act → return)?
- [ ] Information disclosed progressively, each piece timed to its moment of need?
- [ ] Every element justified by the story — anything that doesn't advance it cut?
- [ ] The narrative + a site map exist so the team can see and defend the whole flow?

> Ties to our experience: 02b is built as a literal scroll-journey (water → space → sun), so rules 1, 3, and 5 are the structural backbone — its sticky-pinned sections ARE the chapters, and pacing each reveal to the scroll position is exactly rule 4/5 in practice.
